Road Accident Analysis Dashboard

Project Description

This Road Accident Analysis Dashboard is an interactive data visualization tool designed to analyze and monitor accident trends based on various parameters. The dashboard helps in understanding accident severity, causes, and patterns, aiding authorities in decision-making and improving road safety measures.

Conclusion

  1. Majority of Accidents Are Non-Fatal:

    The highest proportion of casualties (83.49%) are slight injuries, while fatal casualties account for only 2.07%. This indicates that while road accidents are frequent, most do not result in severe injuries or fatalities.

  2. Car Accidents Are the Leading Cause of Casualties:

    80.4% of accidents involve cars, making them the primary contributor to road casualties. Other vehicle types, such as two-wheelers, bicycles, and heavy vehicles, contribute significantly less, but their impact should not be ignored.

  3. Single Carriageways Have the Most Accidents:

    11.4K casualties occur on single carriageways, making them the most accident-prone road type. Infrastructure improvements on these roads can significantly reduce accident rates.

  4. Dry Road Conditions See the Most Accidents:

    Contrary to common belief, most accidents occur on dry roads (7.82K casualties), rather than wet or snowy/icy roads. This suggests that adverse weather is not the primary cause of accidents—driver behavior, road design, and traffic volume may be bigger factors.

  5. Urban Areas Have More Accidents Than Rural Areas:

    11.6K casualties occur in urban areas, compared to 7.2K in rural areas. This is likely due to higher traffic density, pedestrian movement, and intersections in cities. However, rural accidents may be more severe due to higher vehicle speeds.

  6. Most Accidents Happen in Daylight:

    Despite the perception that nighttime driving is more dangerous, 8.4K casualties occur in daylight compared to 6.1K at night. This suggests that factors like traffic congestion, distractions, and reckless driving during peak hours contribute to accidents more than poor visibility at night.
